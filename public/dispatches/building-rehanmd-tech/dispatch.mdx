# Building rehanmd.tech

Every developer portfolio is a statement.

## Why a Train?

I wanted something that felt like movement, the sense that you're going somewhere, that the next stop could change everything. A subway car is liminal space: you're between where you were and where you're going. That's what building software feels like to me.

## The Stack

The scene is built with React Three Fiber, which lets me write Three.js declaratively inside React. The train interior is entirely procedural, no 3D models, just code generating geometry. Every seat, pole, window, and ceiling panel is a `<mesh>` with a `<boxGeometry>` or `<cylinderGeometry>`.

Post-processing adds the atmosphere: bloom makes the tunnel lights glow, vignette darkens the edges, film grain adds texture, and a subtle depth of field blurs the far end of the tunnel. Camera shake makes it feel like the train is actually moving.

## The Philosophy

CS right now sits inside the fastest-moving environment I've ever watched a field operate in. Frameworks rewrite themselves on a quarterly cadence, models double in capability every few months, entire job categories blink into existence and out of relevance inside a year. Trying to win that race by memorizing syntax for a specific language, or by going all in on any single technology, is a losing bet. The half-life of that knowledge is shrinking, and it shrinks faster every year.

So I aim my effort elsewhere. I focus on hammering down heuristics, workflows, mental models, the way I decompose a problem, the way I sequence a debugging pass, the way I read a new codebase. Those transfer to any new tech, any new automation, any tool I haven't seen yet. The frontier moves. The way I move through the frontier doesn't.

> The frontier moves. The way I move through the frontier doesn't.

If I want to thrive in the future, especially the one we are clearly walking into with AI, the work is to be novel, to be creative, and to study human nature. Nobody knows what gets automated next. Nobody knows what the interface for software even looks like in five years. So I bet on the things I think will stay constant: taste, judgment, knowing what humans actually want, knowing how to build something that doesn't feel hollow. The stuff that AI doesn't get to flatten because it can't.

The projects section reflects this. It isn't a grid of cards, it's a metro line map, because every project is a stop on a journey, and the journey is the point. The blog section is a station wall with posters, because ideas are things you encounter in passing. Some catch your eye. Some you walk past. That's fine.

## What's Next

This site will keep evolving. New projects become new stations on the map. New posts become new posters on the wall. The train keeps moving.
